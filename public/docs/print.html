LoopUpward — User Guide

Welcome

LoopUpward is a place to put the things your brain keeps generating — ideas, worries, goals, plans, half-thoughts on a staircase — so they stop circling in your head and start lining up into days you can actually live.

This guide explains every part of the app from your side of the screen: what each section is for, what every button does, and how the pieces connect. Each chapter ends with concrete examples.

The app in one paragraph

You capture a thought the moment it appears (before it evaporates). When a thought keeps coming back, you give it a shape in Life — a goal, a habit, a book you’re reading — and file it under the part of life it belongs to. Plan turns those shapes into concrete days: a short list every morning of what would make today a win. Notes hold the longer thoughts that don’t need doing, and Reflect shows you, with your own real history, that you’re moving.

The idea behind LoopUpward

Thoughts disappear. That’s the whole problem.

If your brain produces more thoughts than you can act on — career worries while brushing your teeth, a gift idea in traffic, “I should really exercise” while climbing stairs — you know the two failure modes:

  1. You forget. The good idea from Tuesday is simply gone.
  2. You don’t forget. Everything stays in your head at once, nothing has an order, and choosing what to do first becomes so heavy that you do nothing. Overthinking paralysis.

LoopUpward is built on a simple deal with your brain: write it down the moment it appears, and you’re allowed to stop thinking about it. The thought isn’t lost — it’s waiting, and there’s a path that takes it from “waiting” to “done.”

The loop

Everything in the app is one repeating loop:

Capture → Organize → Act → Reflect

  • Capture — get the thought out of your head in five seconds. No categories, no decisions.
  • Organize — later, calmly, decide what it is: a goal? a habit? just a note? File it where it belongs.
  • Act — every day, Plan shows a short list. You work the list, not your anxiety.
  • Reflect — the journal and reviews turn your history into proof that the loop works.

You don’t have to use all of it. Capturing alone already helps. But each part feeds the next, and the further a thought travels around the loop, the less it weighs.

One consistent rule set

The app tries hard to behave the same way everywhere, so you learn it once:

  • Everything you create is a “thing” (an item). A goal, a habit, a note, a book — same object, different clothing. Any thing can live inside any other thing, move between areas, and change its shape later. Nothing is locked in.
  • Progress flows upward. Finish a chapter and the book moves; the book moves and the yearly goal moves. You never update a percentage by hand unless you want to.
  • Checking a box means the same thing wherever you see it. One rule, explained in The completion rule.
  • Nothing vanishes silently. Completed items stay visible (struck through), deleted items go to a recoverable Trash, unfinished tasks carry forward without shame.

Getting started in ten minutes

A first session that touches every part of the app once.

1. Capture three thoughts (1 minute)

Open Capture and type whatever is on your mind right now, one thought per entry:

“need to fix my sleep” “mom’s birthday — plan something” “that book about deep work everyone mentions”

That’s it. They’re safe. Notice how your head feels slightly quieter.

2. Make a life area (1 minute)

Open Life → + Area. Areas are the big departments of your life — most people want 3 or 4 to start:

  • 💪 Health
  • 💼 Career
  • ❤️ Family
  • 💰 Money

(The free plan includes 4 areas, which is plenty to begin.)

3. Give one thought a shape (2 minutes)

Back in Capture, take “need to fix my sleep” and organize it. Choose what it is — say, a habit: “In bed by 11pm”, repeating every day, in your Health area.

The moment it has a repeat, it will show up on your day list automatically. You never have to remember it again.

4. Plan today (2 minutes)

Open Plan. Your new habit is already there. Add one or two one-time tasks with “Add something for today…”:

“Call about mom’s birthday” ⭐ Important

That’s a complete day plan. Two or three things. The morning question — “what should I do today?” — now has a written answer.

5. Do one, check one (1 minute)

Do the easiest thing on the list, then tap its checkbox. The progress bar moves. If the task belonged to a goal, the goal moved too — everywhere at once.

6. Close the day (2 minutes)

In the evening, Plan has a small journal on the same screen: how the day went, mood, energy. Two sentences is enough. On Sunday, open Reflect and it will show you the week you actually had.

Capture: emptying your head

Capture is the front door of the app, and the only part you must use fast. A thought lives here as a seed: raw text, no category, no decision made.

Capturing

Type the thought, press enter, done. Capture on the go — waiting for coffee, mid-walk, the second the idea appears. The bar for what “deserves” capturing should be on the floor: if it occupied your mind, it qualifies.

Good seeds look like:

“gift for Ravi — something with cycling?” “why am I always tired at 4pm” “learn enough Spanish for the Mexico trip” “cancel that subscription”

What happens to a seed

Seeds wait in the inbox until you decide, and a seed only ever moves when you move it:

  • Organize it — turn it into a real thing: a goal, habit, note, task for a specific day. The seed archives itself and the new thing lands in its section (the app tells you where, with a link). There’s an undo right after, if you regret the shape you chose.
  • Later — not ready to decide? Park it. It moves out of the inbox but stays reachable.
  • Archive — the thought mattered enough to write but needs nothing. Kept, hidden.
  • Delete — gone, but only ever with a confirmation. Personal thoughts never vanish silently.

The inbox habit

The inbox works best emptied regularly, not instantly. Capture all day without deciding anything; then once a day or every few days, sit down calm and sort. Deciding is a different mental mode than capturing — the app deliberately doesn’t ask you to do both at once.

Notes: longer thoughts

Notes are for writing that isn’t a task: journal-adjacent thinking, lists that aren’t to-do lists, research, recipes, meeting notes, anything with paragraphs. A note never nags you — it has no checkbox, no schedule, no progress bar.

Writing in markdown

Notes are written in markdown: plain text with a few symbols for structure. The editor shows two panes — Write (your markdown) and Preview (how it renders). On a desktop screen both are visible side by side and the preview updates while you type; on a phone, Write and Preview are two tabs above the editor.

Everything supported:

# Big heading
## Medium heading
### Small heading

Normal paragraph text.

**bold** and *italic* and ~~struck out~~ and `code`

- a bullet list
- second bullet

1. a numbered list
2. second entry

> a quote, or something worth setting apart

[a link](https://example.com)

---   (a horizontal line)

Nothing converts behind your back: what you type is exactly what’s stored, and the preview is just a rendering of it. Notes you wrote in the app’s old formatting editor open normally — they’re converted to markdown the first time you edit them.

Folders

Folders group notes: “July 2026”, “Recipes”, “Project X research”. On the Notes screen, folders sit as chips at the top; loose notes float below in a grid.

Filing a note into a folder, three ways:

  1. Drag it — on a computer, drag the card onto a folder chip. On a phone, press and hold the card for a moment (it lifts and buzzes), then slide it onto the chip and let go.
  2. The ⋮ menu on the card → Move to folder.
  3. Select several — tap the small circle on a card to enter selection, tap more cards, then Move to folder moves the whole batch at once.

Deleting a folder never deletes its notes — they become loose notes again.

When a note grows up

Some notes turn out to be plans in disguise. Organize into something (on the note’s page) creates a real goal/habit/task from it — the note itself stays exactly where it was, unchanged. The note was thinking; the new thing is doing.

Examples

  • A packing list — one note, bullets, done. It doesn’t need to be twelve tasks.
  • “Thoughts on quitting my job” — a note this month. Maybe a goal next month. Organize into something when it’s time.
  • Book quotes — a “Reading” folder, one note per book, > quotes inside.

Life: everything you’re growing

Life is the map. Where Plan asks “what today?”, Life asks “what, at all?” — every goal, habit, and project you’re carrying, arranged by the part of life it serves.

Areas

Areas are the top level: Health, Career, Family, Money, French, whatever your life is actually made of. Each area page lists everything filed under it with a progress bar per item. Items don’t need an area (“not filed anywhere yet” collects the loose ones), but areas are what make five hundred ambitions feel like six shelves.

Things (items)

Everything you create is the same kind of object wearing different clothes. When you add something you pick what it is:

  • Goal — has an end. “Run a half marathon.” Done when it’s done.
  • Habit — has no end. “Meditate.” Lives on repeats; measured in streaks.
  • Routine — a habit with a script. “Morning routine” holds ordered steps, each optionally timed (“face wash 5 min, meditation 15, brush 5”), and stays one single entry on Today — the steps never clutter your list. It keeps its own visible hours (a night routine can wait until 9 pm), the step timer walks the script one step at a time, and it’s checked off per day like a habit, with streaks. Whole chapter: Routines.
  • List — checkable contents under one name. “Groceries”, “Push day”, “People to thank.” Entries can carry amounts (“2 kg”, “₹500”) that add up per unit on the list’s total line, they stay ticked once done (↺ Untick all resets a reusable list), and the ticked share is the list’s progress. The whole list is still one node — entries never clutter Life or Today. Lists live at /lists, linked from the Plan page.
  • Project — a container of steps. “Renovate kitchen.”
  • Book — tracked chapter by chapter.
  • Milestone — a marker inside something bigger. “First 10k run.”
  • Note / Folder — live in Notes (see that chapter).
  • Quotes, principles, lessons, memories, ideas, dreams — keepsakes; they live in a quiet space of their own, out of the execution flow.

Don’t overthink the choice — you can change kind, area, parent, and everything else later. Nothing is locked.

Nesting: things inside things

Any thing can live inside any other. This is the app’s spine:

🎯 Reach conversational Spanish        (year)
   🎯 Finish beginner course           (this quarter)
      📖 Textbook, 20 chapters         (book: chapter 9/20)
   🔁 15 minutes of practice           (habit, daily)

Progress flows upward automatically. A thing with its own tracker reports that; a thing without one averages its children. Tick the textbook forward and the course, and the year goal above it, both move. You never do bookkeeping upward.

And completion climbs too: finishing a thing nested inside a metered parent ticks that parent’s meter by one. Split a 3-part book into three nested targets — completing part 1 makes the book read 1/3; completing the last part completes the book, which ticks “read 6 books” up by one, all the way up the chain, each move written into history like hand-entered progress.

Open any item to see what’s inside it, its history, streaks and heatmap (for habits), and the “break off a piece for today” box — the bridge to Plan, covered in the next chapter.

Moving things

Move on an item’s page relocates it: pick a life area and/or the thing it should live inside. The list shows everything you have (goals and projects first, searchable) with each candidate’s area and time horizon, so you can tell your yearly goals apart. Picking a parent also adopts that parent’s area, which is almost always what you meant — override it with the chips if not. Nothing moves until you press Move; Cancel really cancels.

Completed, someday, trash

  • Mark complete retires a thing with dignity — struck through, still visible, reopenable.
  • Someday shelves a thing you’re not working on without deleting the ambition.
  • Delete moves it to Trash (in Settings), recoverable for 7 days (30 with Premium). Children of a deleted thing move up a level — they aren’t lost.

Plan: today, and further out

Plan is the control room. It answers one question at five zoom levels: what am I doing — today, this week, this month, this quarter, this year?

The Today list

The default view is today: a short list of concrete actions, a progress bar, and the day’s journal beside it. Things land on today four ways:

  1. You add one directly — “Add something for today…”: a title, optionally a date (plant a task on any future day — a birthday, a deadline), a priority star, a small note. If you give it a repeat instead, it becomes a routine and files itself under a life area of your choice.
  2. A repeat puts it there — anything with a schedule (every day, weekdays, Mon/Wed/Fri, 4× a week, monthly) appears on its days automatically. This is the “set it once, never think again” path.
  3. You break a piece off a goal — on any goal’s page, “break off a piece” creates a small task linked to that goal, for today, tomorrow, or any day you pick — lay a week of chapters out in one sitting, or pull next week’s forward when today has slack. For metered goals (counters, books) you say how much the piece is worth — “Read 2 chapters” moves the meter by 2 when done, and 0 is a real choice: “clone the repo” adds no PRs to a PR goal, but it moves you in the direction, and it still lives under the goal. The same link is available from “Add something for today…” via Counts toward a goal, and on Today the piece names its goal (“↳ finish part 2…”) so you always know whose little part it is.
  4. A goal is marked for today — setting a thing’s planning horizon to Today keeps it on the list until you complete it.

Reading a row

  • Square checkbox — one-time thing; checking completes it (and the goal it belongs to, when it was the goal’s last open piece).
  • Round checkbox — a repeat; checking logs today and feeds the streak. Tomorrow it returns. See The completion rule.
  • ⭐ — priority. +2 chapters — how much this task moves its goal’s meter. “3 steps · 25 min” — a routine wearing its script’s total length (“1/3 steps” once you’re mid-way); its timer runs the steps one at a time, in order. Routines with set hours only appear inside them — before then they wait under a quiet “Out of hours” line. “carried from Mon” — an unfinished task following you, without shame, until done. Finishing it on a later day keeps the record honest (“done Wed” shows on its planned day).
  • The little icons: open the linked goal, plan what a habit means today (“clean” → “clean desk”), edit, focus timer, remove.

Completing a task never reshuffles the list — rows move only when you drag them or use Sort (unfinished first, custom order, hide completed).

The focus timer

The timer icon on any row opens a full-screen timer for that one task — for when starting is the hard part. Finish, check it off from inside, move to the next.

Week, month, quarter, year

The tabs across the top hold standing lists for each period, navigable like a calendar (July, August… 2026, 2027…). Anything whose planning horizon is “this week” appears in that specific week’s list — with its progress, and a → Today button that pulls it onto the day when you’re ready to act.

These lists are the planning layer: on Sunday, look at the week list; each morning, pull from it. Nothing here nags — it waits.

Browsing days

The week strip and Yesterday / Today / Tomorrow jumps let you look back (what did Tuesday look like?) or plan ahead (load Saturday with errands). Only the real today shows carried-over tasks; other days show just what belongs to them.

The journal on the same screen

The right side of today (below, on a phone) is the daily journal: a “thinking about…” box for the morning, an end-of-day reflection, mood and energy. It lives here, not in a separate app section, because closing the day is part of the day. Reflect (next chapters) is built from what you write here.

Routines: the parts of the day that run on rails

A routine is a timetable. You write it once — an ordered list of steps, each with the minutes it takes, if you want them — and from then on it just shows up. A morning routine, a night routine, gym, study: sequences you do the same way every time, that shouldn’t cost you a single planning thought again.

What a routine is made of

  • A name — “Morning”, “Night”, “Gym”, anything.
  • A script — ordered steps: wash face (5), meditation (15), brush teeth (5). The order matters; drag steps up and down by their handle. Minutes are optional per step — “take meds” has no duration, and that’s fine.
  • Visible hours — from when to when it sits on your Today list. A morning routine set to 5:00 am – 12:00 pm disappears after noon; a night routine set to 9:00 pm – 2:00 am shows up in the evening (an end before the start simply wraps past midnight). Outside its hours it waits under a quiet “Out of hours” line — never a nagging task. No hours set means it shows all day.
  • The night belongs to the evening it started. For a routine whose hours wrap past midnight, anything you do before 4 am (or before its window ends, if that’s later) counts for the previous day — ticking your night routine at 1 am keeps the log and the streak on the night it belongs to, and it won’t count as tomorrow’s routine done in advance.
  • A schedule — routines repeat daily unless you say otherwise, and they’re checked off per day with a streak, exactly like habits.

The minutes add up (5 + 15 + 5 = 25 min) and Today shows the routine wearing its length: “3 steps · 25 min”.

One line on Today, always

Steps never appear in your todo list. The whole routine is one entry with a round checkbox; the steps live inside it. Your day list stays short even if your morning script has nine steps.

There are three ways to work through one:

  1. Tick the round checkbox on Today — logs the whole day at once, no questions.
  2. Tick steps one by one on the Routines page (the 🌄 Routines link above the day’s list). Ticking the last step logs the day by itself — and un-ticking a step takes the log back. Mid-way through, the Today row shows “1/3 steps”.
  3. Run it with the step timer — the good one.

The step runner

Open the routine’s focus timer (the clock icon on its row, or ▶ Run on the Routines page) and press Start the routine. It walks the script in order:

  • Each timed step gets its own countdown. Finishing a step rolls straight into the next one’s timer — no fiddling between steps.
  • A step with no minutes saved asks once: give it a length, or choose “no timer, just show it” — the step stays on screen (with a quiet count-up) until you mark it done. Meds don’t take five minutes; they take until you’ve taken them.
  • Skip for now sends the current step to the back of the line instead of pretending it happened. It comes around again at the end.
  • Progress is saved per step, per day — closing the runner mid-way loses nothing, and the ticks are already on every other device.
  • The last step’s check logs the routine’s day and feeds the streak.

Where routines live

The Routines page (linked from Today) lists them all — each with its hours, steps, minutes and streak, tickable on the spot. New ones start from a template (morning, night) or from scratch: name it, give it hours, then write its script on its page. A routine is a normal life node too — it can live in an area, and “Retire routine” ends it with its history intact.

One goal, top to bottom — a worked example

The promise: if I do my small list today, I am provably moving toward my year goal. Here is the full setup, once, so the shape is clear. It takes about three minutes.

The ambition

“I want to read 5 books this year.”

Build the ladder

1. The year goal. In Life, add a goal to your Growth area:

  • Read 5 books in 2026 — kind Goal, tracker Counter, target 5, unit “books”, planning horizon This year.

2. The current book. Add a thing inside it:

  • Finish “Linchpin” — kind Book, tracker chapter 0 / 20, horizon This month.

3. This week’s slice. You want 7 chapters this week. Open the book, break off a piece for today:

  • “Read chapter 1” — moves the meter by 1.

That’s the whole setup. No week-goal object is even required — the book’s meter is the week’s scoreboard. (If you like an explicit weekly target, nest a counter “7 chapters this week”, horizon This week, inside the book. The horizon stepper knows a week inside a month stays inside that month.)

Live a day of it

Tomorrow morning, Plan shows Read chapter 1 with a “+1 chapters” tag. You read, you check it:

  • the task completes,
  • Linchpin becomes chapter 1/20 — visible in Life, in the month list, everywhere,
  • Read 5 books in 2026 nudges forward, because progress flows upward through the nesting.

Each day, break off the next chapter — or set a repeat on the book (“weekdays”) so a reading row appears by itself and you log chapters as you go.

Finish a book

When the meter hits 20/20 the book completes itself. Bump the year counter: 1 of 5 books. Add the next book inside the year goal and the ladder keeps going.

When to trace, and when not to

Not everything deserves this. “Give clothes for washing” is a task on today, full stop. The ladder is for the three or four ambitions where you genuinely want the answer to “is today connected to anything?” to be yes — and visible.

The completion rule

One rule, learned once, true everywhere:

Checking a thing checks the thing itself — everywhere it appears. A square checkbox completes the thing for good. A round checkbox logs this day of a repeating thing.

Square: one-time things

Tasks, goals, milestones. Check one — on Today, in a Life area, inside a parent goal, in a week list — and it is done in all of those places at once, struck through but still visible. Check again to undo. The same tap as the Mark complete button on the item’s own page.

And it flows down and up:

  • Completing a task that was the last open piece of a goal completes the goal too. You did the thing; the app does the filing.
  • Completing a child updates the parent’s percentage — a parent measured by its children moves the moment any of them does.

Round: repeating things

Habits and anything with a schedule. The circle means “done today”: it feeds the streak and the heatmap, and tomorrow the circle returns empty, because the habit isn’t finished — that’s the point of a habit. Some repeats count more than once a day (“3 glasses of water”) — the circle fills as a ring, 2 of 3.

Ending a habit forever is a deliberate act (Retire habit on its page), never a side effect of doing it today.

Why both exist

If one checkbox meant both “done forever” and “done today,” you couldn’t trust it — doing your pushups once would delete the pushups habit. The shape tells you what your tap will mean before you tap.

This is also why a thing can’t simultaneously repeat and be marked “do today”: the two give the checkbox different meanings, so setting one clears the other (a repeat reaches Today by itself anyway).

The two symbols below are the two checkbox shapes you’ll see in the app itself: ☐ square (one-time — completing) and ◯ round (repeating — logging today).

Reflect: journal and reviews

Reflect is where the loop closes: the app takes the days you actually lived and shows them back to you.

The daily journal

Lives right on the Today screen (and under Reflect):

  • “Today I’m thinking about…” — a morning brain-dump box. Unstructured on purpose.
  • End of day — what went well, what to try differently. Two sentences beat zero.
  • Mood and energy — a 1–5 tap each. Thirty days of these dots teach you more about your 4pm slump than any productivity article.

One entry per day; everything is editable later. Journal pages can be exported.

Reviews

A review assembles a period — week, month (and quarter, year with Premium) — from your real history:

  • what you completed, and which life areas it served,
  • habit consistency (days showing up, streaks),
  • meters that moved: pages, rupees, kilometers, chapters,
  • your mood/energy pattern,
  • a free-writing box for the review itself.

Navigate periods like a calendar — last week, March, Q2. From any week/month list in Plan, “Reflect on this week →” jumps to the matching review.

Unfinished ≠ failure

Reviews deliberately don’t scold. An unfinished plan is information: the week was overloaded, or the goal is mis-sized, or that habit needs a different time of day. The point of reflecting is to adjust the next loop, not to grade the last one.

A five-minute Sunday

  1. Open Reflect → this week.
  2. Read what happened (it’s already assembled).
  3. Write three lines: proudest thing, hardest thing, one change for next week.
  4. Open Plan → Week tab → next week, and place that one change in it.

That’s a complete personal review system, five minutes, every week.

(Free accounts see roughly the last 12 weeks of history; Premium keeps all of it, plus quarterly and yearly reviews.)

Settings, sync and your data

Your data, two modes

  • On this device — without signing in, everything lives in your browser. Fully functional, fully private, but tied to that one browser.
  • Synced — sign in with Google and your data lives in your private cloud space: phone and laptop see the same life.

Keeping devices fresh

When you’re signed in, edits made on one device reach another two ways:

  • Automatically — returning to the app (switching back to the tab, waking the laptop) pulls the latest by itself.
  • The sync button 🔄 — top-right on a phone, next to the logo on a desktop. Tap it to fetch right now; it spins while fetching and shows a ✓ when you’re current. No browser refresh needed, ever.

Without an account the button simply re-reads this device’s storage (useful with two tabs open).

Simple mode

“How the app feels”: Simple / Full. Simple keeps the surface to to-dos, notes and your day: adding something asks only for a name, when it appears, and where it belongs; Reflect steps out of the nav. Every advanced tool — types, progress meters, labels, reviews — stays one tap away under More options, and switching back to Full is instant. Nothing is ever deleted by the switch; complexity is hidden, not removed.

Give Simple to the friend who “just wants a to-do app.” They’re using the same engine.

Appearance

Light/dark theme, and a choice of display fonts for headings and quotes.

Trash

Deleted items rest in Trash, restorable for 7 days (free) or 30 days (Premium), then they’re gone for good. Deleting a parent never deletes its children — they step up a level.

Export

Export everything downloads your entire life as one JSON file: every item, seed, action, log, journal entry. It’s yours — feed it to a spreadsheet, an AI tool, an archive. No lock-in.

FAQ and gotchas

I checked a habit off. Why isn’t it “completed” in Life? Round checkbox = “done today”, and today is logged (streak, heatmap). A habit never completes by checkbox — it repeats; that’s its job. To end it for good, open it and Retire habit. One-time things (square checkbox) do complete everywhere at once. See The completion rule.

I set something to “Today” — does it also need a repeat? No. Today alone puts it on the list until you complete it. A repeat alone puts it there on schedule. They’re mutually exclusive on purpose — each gives the checkbox a different meaning, so choosing one clears the other.

Something I expected isn’t in this week’s list. Week/month/quarter/year lists are real calendar instances. A goal tagged “this week” last week sits in last week’s list — step the arrows back and you’ll find it, then → Today or re-schedule it into the current week. Old items from before this existed appear in the current period.

Yesterday’s unfinished task is showing today. Bug? Feature: tasks carry forward until done (“carried from Mon”), only on the real today. Finish it today and both days stay honest: planned Monday, done Wednesday.

Where did my completed things go? Nowhere — completed items stay in their lists, struck through. Lists with a lot done offer Hide completed under Sort.

I moved something and the area changed by itself. Picking a parent adopts the parent’s life area — living inside something means living where it lives. Override with the area chips in the same Move sheet before pressing Move; nothing applies until then.

What’s the difference between a note and a “someday” goal? A note is thinking (no checkbox, no guilt). Someday is a shelved intention — still a goal, just not now. If it will eventually be done, make it a goal; if it’s words you want to keep, it’s a note.

My phone edit isn’t on my laptop. Both signed in? Then tap the 🔄 sync button (or just refocus the tab — it pulls automatically). Not signed in = each device is its own island by design.

Markdown symbols are showing as plain text. You’re looking at the Write pane — tap Preview (or widen the window; desktop shows both). The symbols are supposed to stay visible where you type.

Can I turn a note into a goal? A goal into a habit? Yes and yes. Organize into something on a note; Edit on anything else. Nothing about a thing is permanent — kind, tracker, area, parent, horizon all change anytime.

What do the free limits actually stop? Creating a 5th area or a 41st active item. Completed and someday items don’t count against you. Everything you already made keeps working.

I deleted something by accident. Settings → Trash → restore, within 7 days (30 on Premium).
